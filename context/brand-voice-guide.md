# Brand Voice Guide
## RawIntel

---

## Brand Personality

RawIntel sounds like the smartest person in your group chat — the one who actually watched the game, read the injury report, and checked the line movement before weighing in. They don't tell you what to do. They hand you the real picture and trust you to make the call. They're not trying to impress you. They're trying to be useful.

**The brand is:** direct, calm, honest, specific, restrained
**The brand is NOT:** hype-driven, bro-coded, salesy, vague, performatively confident

---

## Tone Spectrum

| Dimension | Position (1–5) | What this means in practice |
|---|---|---|
| Formal ←————→ Casual | 3 | Not corporate. Not bro-speak. Conversational but considered — like a smart friend who takes this seriously |
| Reserved ←————→ Bold | 4 | Direct declarative statements. No hedging. Says the uncomfortable thing plainly if it's true |
| Serious ←————→ Playful | 2 | The subject involves money. Warmth yes, jokes no. Dry wit is acceptable; enthusiasm about picks is never acceptable |
| Technical ←————→ Simple | 2 | Complex data, plain language. Specificity is mandatory ("26.9 PPG" not "productive lately"). Complexity gets translated, not dumbed down |
| Polished ←————→ Raw | 3.5 | Leans raw. Unadorned. No marketing gloss. The product's honesty is the brand's honesty |

## Voice Rules

### Rule 1: Short sentences. Fragments are tools, not mistakes.
The brand speaks in punchy, declarative statements. A sentence ends when the thought is complete — not when you've filled a line. Fragments create emphasis and rhythm. Use them deliberately.

**Do:** "The upgrade is not a calculation. It's an exhale."
**Don't:** "Users often find that the decision to upgrade to Pro feels intuitive once they've experienced the quality of the analysis."

---

### Rule 2: Specificity over generality. Always.
Vague copy is the enemy. Every claim should be a specific claim. Every stat should be the actual number. The reader scans for tells that you're AI slop. Specificity is what proves you're not.

**Do:** "Embiid at 26.9 PPG and 7.7 RPG on the road. Here's what that means tonight."
**Don't:** "Embiid has been productive lately, which could impact tonight's result."

---

### Rule 3: The reader owns their decision. Always return it to them.
The brand never tells people what to do. The product hands over the reasoning; the user holds the conclusion. Every piece of copy should reinforce this — even implicitly. If a sentence could be read as a recommendation, rewrite it.

**Do:** "That's what the data says. Your call."
**Don't:** "Based on this analysis, the under looks like strong value tonight."

---

### Rule 4: Contrast is the structure. Use "Not X. Y." freely.
RawIntel was built in opposition to what existed before. The brand voice reflects that — it often defines itself by what it isn't before stating what it is. This is not humility; it's precision.

**Do:** "Not a picks service. Not a stats dump. The read between them."
**Don't:** "RawIntel offers a unique and differentiated approach to sports betting analysis."

---

### Rule 5: Address the reader as "you." Make the scenario immediate.
Write in second person. Put the reader in the moment. Present tense makes scenarios feel live, not hypothetical. The reader should feel like you're describing their exact Wednesday night.

**Do:** "You've used your free breakdown. It's 9:45pm. Late games are tipping off. You have money on one."
**Don't:** "Users who have exhausted their free daily breakdown may find themselves wanting additional analysis for evening games."

---

### Rule 6: Never manufacture confidence. Earn it or admit you don't have it.
The brand's competitive advantage is honesty about uncertainty. Fragile reads exist for a reason. In copy, this means: don't oversell, don't promise outcomes, don't use language that implies certainty you can't back up. State what you know. Name what you don't.

**Do:** "This one's messy. Fragile read. Here's why."
**Don't:** "Our expert analysis gives you everything you need to make tonight's best bets."

---

### Rule 7: Earn authority through the founder's story, not credentials.
RawIntel has no capper track record and never will. The credibility is Kim's story: built the tool because she needed it and nothing else existed. That's more powerful than any W/L stat for this audience. When authority is needed, reach for the story, not the superlative.

**Do:** "I built RawIntel because I got tired of products that lied to me."
**Don't:** "RawIntel's expert team brings years of sports betting analysis experience."

---

### Rule 8: No shame, no hype. Calm confidence only.
This audience carries guilt about betting and skepticism about betting products. The brand's job is to be the calm, non-judgmental presence in a category full of noise. No BIG WIN energy. No "lock of the day." No "crush the books." Visual and tonal restraint isn't minimalism — it's user safety.

**Do:** "Betting should feel like a thinking person's hobby."
**Don't:** "It's time to start winning. Get your edge."

## Vocabulary

### Always Use

- **"breakdown"** — the product unit. Always lowercase. "Run a breakdown." "Read the breakdown." Never "analysis report" or "prediction."
- **"read"** — as in "a clean read," "what's the read," "the read on this game." Specific to RawIntel's product language.
- **"data"** — not "stats," not "analytics," not "metrics." The brand promises raw data. Use the word.
- **"decision"** — always belongs to the user. "Your decision." "You decide." This word keeps the contract intact.
- **"framework"** — when describing the six-step process. Signals structure and repeatability.
- **"Fragile" / "Clear Spot"** — always capitalize. These are product vocabulary, not generic adjectives.
- **"Signal Grade"** — capitalize. Product term.
- **"deliberate"** — the word for what users become: deliberate bettors. Not "better bettors." Not "winners."
- **"you"** — address the reader directly, always. The brand speaks to one person, not an audience.
- **"plain English"** — use exactly this phrase when describing what RawIntel produces. It's a promise the product keeps.

### Never Use

- **"AI-powered" / "AI-generated" / "powered by AI"** — triggers the AI slop defense. Lead with what the product does, never how it works.
- **"lock" / "lock of the day" / "locks"** — picks service language. Incompatible with the brand.
- **"guaranteed" / "guaranteed winners"** — legal liability and brand poison.
- **"picks"** — when describing what RawIntel produces. It produces breakdowns and reads, not picks.
- **"win more" / "start winning"** — outcome promise we explicitly don't make. Use "bet deliberately" or "understand the game."
- **"level up" / "sharpen your edge" / "crush the books" / "beat the books"** — bro-coded sports performance language. Alienates the She Bets audience and violates the tone.
- **"insights"** — overused, content-marketing filler. Say specifically what you mean.
- **"game-changing" / "revolutionary" / "cutting-edge"** — hype language. The brand earns trust through understatement, not overstatement.
- **"expert" / "experts"** — positions RawIntel as an authority dispensing expertise. The brand is a framework, not an expert.
- **"tips"** — too close to "tipster." Use "analysis" or "breakdown" instead.
- **"smart money"** — implies insider advantage the brand doesn't claim and can't deliver.
- **"leverage"** — corporate jargon.
- **"responsible betting"** — avoid health/addiction framing entirely. Link ncpgambling.org in the footer; don't make wellness claims in copy.

### Industry Terms: Embrace and Explain

Betting vocabulary is fine — it signals the brand is talking to real bettors, not casual fans. Use terms like spread, line, line movement, back-door cover, action, juice, sharp money, public money, fade freely. When introducing a term that a newer bettor might not know, the Line Translator is the resource to point them to. Don't over-explain in copy — that's condescending. Trust that the ICP knows what a spread is.

## Sentence & Structure Patterns

**Sentence length:** Short to medium. Favor under 20 words. Mix 5-word punches with 15-word clarifiers. Never a sentence so long the reader has to re-read it.
**Fragments:** Yes — for emphasis and rhythm. "Not a picks service." "That's the gap." "Your call."
**Questions:** Yes — to open content or to put the reader in the scenario. "What does the data actually say?" "Why am I rationing it?"
**Contractions:** Always. "You're" not "you are." "It's" not "it is." Contractions are what make the brand sound human.
**Person:** Second person "you" as default. First person "I" for Kim's founder voice. First person "we" is rare and only when speaking as the product team.
**Paragraph length:** 1–3 sentences. Air between thoughts. Dense paragraphs signal to the reader that they're being lectured. This brand is a conversation.

## Content Opening Patterns

1. **Put the reader in a specific moment**
   "Wednesday night. 9:45pm. Late games are tipping off."

2. **Declarative contrast statement**
   "Not a picks service. Not a stats dump. The read between them."

3. **Direct question that names the frustration**
   "Tired of picks with no reasoning and stats with no context?"

4. **Bold declarative that names the product's premise**
   "RawIntel never tells you what to bet."

5. **The problem, plainly stated**
   "Every breakdown you've ever read was designed to tell you what to think."

---

## Content Closing Patterns

1. **Return the decision to the user**
   "This is not a pick. This is what the data says. Your decision is always yours."

2. **Low-pressure CTA that feels like an invitation, not a push**
   "Run tonight's breakdown →"
   "See the full slate →"
   "Read the breakdown →"
   *(Never: "Get started today!" "Sign up now!" "Don't miss out!")*

3. **A single sentence that lands the brand promise**
   "That's what the data says. Your call."
   "Bet deliberately."

## Emotional Target

**Reader should feel after consuming this content:**
Calm. Sharp. Like the smartest person in their group chat tonight. Not overwhelmed by data — equipped by it. Not told what to do — trusted to decide. The feeling is: *I understand this game. I'm ready.*

**Emotional posture of the brand:**
Authority without arrogance. The friend who actually did the homework, not the guy who acts like they did. Honest to the point of occasionally naming uncertainty out loud. That honesty is the trust signal, not a weakness.

## Rewrite Examples

### Example 1: Product Description

**Generic version:**
"RawIntel is an AI-powered sports betting analytics platform that provides data-driven insights to help you make smarter betting decisions."

**Brand voice version:**
"RawIntel turns live game data into plain-English breakdowns — six steps, no picks, your call every time."

---

### Example 2: Pain Point Acknowledgment

**Generic version:**
"We understand that navigating the complex world of sports betting information can be overwhelming."

**Brand voice version:**
"You've read the takes. You've seen the locks. Nobody ever shows you the reasoning. That's the part RawIntel doesn't skip."

---

### Example 3: Upgrade CTA

**Generic version:**
"Upgrade to Pro today and unlock unlimited access to our full suite of analytics tools!"

**Brand voice version:**
"You already trust the read. Pro just means you get one for every game tonight. No limits. Full archive. $9.99 a month. Cancel anytime."

---

### Example 4: Email Subject Line

**Generic version:**
"Tonight's Games Are Ready — Don't Miss Out!"

**Brand voice version:**
"Tonight's slate is up."

---

### Example 5: Acknowledging Uncertainty (Fragile Read)

**Generic version:**
"Due to some uncertainty in the data, we recommend caution with this matchup."

**Brand voice version:**
"This one's Fragile. Booker's status is unclear, injury data is stale, and the line's been moving in both directions. We're not pretending to have a clean read when we don't."

## Quick Reference Card

When writing for RawIntel, ask yourself:
- [ ] Does this sound like the smartest person in the group chat — direct, specific, non-hype?
- [ ] Did I avoid picks language, win-promise language, and AI-hype language?
- [ ] Is the reader's decision still theirs at the end of this?
- [ ] Is every claim specific enough that a knowledgeable bettor won't roll their eyes?
- [ ] Would this make someone feel sharp and trusted — not sold to?
- [ ] If this is a fact — is it checkable and correct?
